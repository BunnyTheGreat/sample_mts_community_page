Dyshidrotic Eczema

Dyshidrotic eczema, also known as dyshidrosis or pompholyx, is characterized by small, fluid-filled blisters that develop on the palms of the hands, sides of the fingers, and soles of the feet. These blisters are often intensely itchy or painful and may dry out, peel, and return in recurring cycles. Although the exact cause is unknown, flares are commonly associated with stress, warm weather, frequent handwashing, sweating, or exposure to certain metals and irritants. Because it affects the hands and feet, dyshidrotic eczema can make everyday activities especially uncomfortable.

Understanding Dyshidrotic Eczema: The 5 W's

Who Gets It? Primarily affects adults (20-40 years old), though it can occur in children. More common in people with atopic dermatitis or a family history of eczema. Slightly more common in women than men.
What Causes It? Exact cause is unknown, but triggers include stress, contact with irritants (metals like nickel, strong detergents), moisture exposure, and nickel sensitivity. Genetic predisposition and immune system dysfunction play a role.
Where Does It Appear? Primarily on the palms and sides of fingers, between fingers, soles of feet, and edges of toes. Rarely appears on other body parts. The blisters are typically very small (1-3mm) and often cluster together.
When Does It Occur? Often worse in spring and autumn. Can flare during stressful periods or with increased moisture exposure (sweating, frequent handwashing). Initial blistering phase typically lasts 2-3 weeks.
How Long Does It Last? Episodes can last 3-4 weeks before blisters dry and scaling occurs. Full healing takes 2-3 months. Many people experience recurring episodes, sometimes seasonally or year-round if triggers aren't identified.

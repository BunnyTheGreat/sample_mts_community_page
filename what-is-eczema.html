What is Eczema? Understanding the Basics

By Mind the Skin Labs • July 16, 2026 • 8 min read

If you've ever dealt with itchy, dry, inflamed skin that just won't seem to go away, you're not alone. Eczema affects millions of people, yet it remains widely misunderstood. This guide breaks down the basics so you can feel more confident about what eczema is and how it happens.

Eczema overview illustration

What is it?

Eczema isn’t just “dry skin.” It's a type of inflammatory skin condition called dermatitis, which can make your skin dry, itchy, and bumpy. With eczema-impacted skin, the skin barrier is weakened, letting important moisture escape and making the skin more vulnerable to irritants.

Think of your skin as a brick wall. Healthy skin has tightly packed "bricks" (skin cells) held together by "mortar" (natural oils and proteins). With eczema, the mortar is weaker than it should be. That means the barrier can’t do its job as effectively, allowing moisture to escape while letting irritants, allergens, and bacteria in.

"Eczema is a condition of your skin barrier and immune response — not a sign of poor hygiene or something you can catch from someone else."

Who is impacted?

While eczema often begins during infancy or early childhood, people of all ages can develop it, including teenagers and adults. Around 1 in 10 people will experience eczema at some point in their lives.

More than 31 million Americans are affected by some form of eczema, and it can look different depending on skin tone. On darker skin, eczema may appear purple, gray, or dark brown rather than bright red. No matter who you are, eczema is common, and you're far from alone.

What can cause it?

Eczema triggers vary from person to person, but many people react more strongly to everyday factors. Here are the most common causes:

  • Environmental factors: Fragrance, pollen, harsh soaps, weather changes, and stress can all prompt inflammation and cause a flare.
  • Genetics: Eczema often runs in families. If you or a close relative has eczema, asthma, or seasonal allergies, you're more likely to develop atopic dermatitis.
  • Mental health and stress: High stress, anxiety, or depression can make flare-ups more frequent.

Symptoms

Everyone experiences eczema differently, but some of the most common symptoms include:

  • Persistent itching
  • Dry or flaky skin
  • Red, purple, gray, or brown patches of inflamed skin
  • Rough or thickened areas from repeated scratching
  • Cracking or peeling skin
  • Small bumps that may ooze or crust

Symptoms can be mild to severe and often come in cycles. You may have calm weeks followed by sudden flare-ups.

The Bottom Line

Living with eczema can be uncomfortable and emotionally draining, but understanding what's happening beneath your skin can make it feel less overwhelming. Eczema isn't contagious and it isn't caused by poor hygiene. It's a complex condition involving your skin barrier, immune system, and genetics.

Although there isn't a one-size-fits-all solution, there are many ways to support healthier skin and reduce flares. Education is a powerful first step.

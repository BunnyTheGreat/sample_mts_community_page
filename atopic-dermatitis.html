Atopic Dermatitis

Atopic dermatitis (AD) is the most common type of eczema, accounting for about 80% of all eczema cases. It is a chronic inflammatory skin condition that causes dry, itchy, and inflamed skin, with symptoms that often come and go in flares. While it usually begins in infancy or childhood, it can develop at any age. Atopic dermatitis is linked to a weakened skin barrier, an overactive immune response, and genetic factors, and it commonly occurs alongside asthma and seasonal allergies.

Understanding Atopic Dermatitis: The 5 W's

Who Gets It? Children (often before age 5) and adults. More common in people with family history of eczema, asthma, or hay fever. Affects about 1 in 10 people worldwide.
What Causes It? A combination of genetic factors and environmental triggers. Issues include impaired skin barrier function, immune system overreaction to irritants, and reduced skin moisture retention.
Where Does It Appear? Commonly on the face, hands, feet, inner elbows, and behind the knees. In infants, often on the cheeks and scalp. Can appear anywhere on the body.
When Does It Occur? Often appears in childhood (ages 6 months to 5 years) but can develop later. Symptoms may worsen in cold, dry seasons and improve in warmer, humid weather.
How Long Does It Last? Chronic and long-lasting. Many children improve by adulthood, but some symptoms may persist throughout life. Requires ongoing management and skincare routines.

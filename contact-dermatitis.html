Contact Dermatitis

Contact dermatitis is a type of eczema that develops when the skin comes into contact with a substance that causes irritation or triggers an allergic reaction. Common triggers include soaps, detergents, fragrances, metals like nickel, and certain plants such as poison ivy. Symptoms often appear only where the skin touched the trigger and may include redness, itching, swelling, or small blisters. Once the irritant or allergen is identified and avoided, symptoms usually improve and future flares can often be prevented.

Understanding Contact Dermatitis: The 5 W's

Who Gets It? Anyone can develop contact dermatitis, regardless of age or genetics. It's particularly common in people with occupational exposure (healthcare workers, cleaners, florists). About 15-20% of the general population experiences it at some point.
What Causes It? Direct contact with irritants (soaps, detergents, harsh chemicals) or allergens (nickel, fragrances, latex, certain plants). Irritant contact dermatitis is more common than allergic contact dermatitis.
Where Does It Appear? Typically where the skin came into contact with the triggering substance. Commonly on hands, face, neck, and areas with thinner skin. The pattern often matches the contact area.
When Does It Occur? Can develop immediately or within 24-48 hours of exposure. Allergic contact dermatitis may take days to appear after first exposure. Symptoms typically resolve within 2-3 weeks of avoiding the trigger.
How Long Does It Last? Short-term if the irritant is avoided. Acute reactions usually resolve in 2-4 weeks. Can become chronic if exposure continues. Some sensitivities may persist indefinitely once developed.
